Bechdel Test: Female representation in film industry has yet to be improved

A look into the Bechdel Test dataset, a female representation measure in film



Since the viral spread of the MeToo movement in October 2017 sparked by sexual abuse allegations against producer Harvey Weinstein, female representation has become an even more discussed issue in Hollywood. The controversy over wage gaps, unequal treatment and sexual harassment in the film scene has been ongoing even before the movement and has gained more momentum in the recent years.

Whether the treatment of actors and actresses in the industry and workplace has been improved is still an area of question. Meanwhile, has the actual representation of women depicted on the screen made progress?

This question has been often answered in terms of the ratio of female characters in films. According to Women and Hollywood, an organization advocating gender diversity in Hollywood, 40 films out of the top-grossing 100 in 2018 featured a female in the leading or co-leading role . This is an 8 percentage point increase compared to 2017 and 20 percentage point increase compared to 2007.

Does the bare increase in the number of female roles in films signify an increased representation of women in the films? Maybe. Are women well-represented and respected in the plots of these fictions? No, according to the Bechdel Test. The Bechdel test aims to assess in detail the presence of females in films as an independent human figure not relative to men. Named after cartoonist Alison Bechdel, whose comic strip the test appears in, the test asks two simple questions; One, “Are there at least two women in the movie having a conversation with each other?”, and two, “Is it about a man?”

Based on the answers to these two questions, the test gives one of the following four scores to films:

  • 0 : No two women are present in the same scene throughout the movie
  • 1 : No two women speak to each other throughout the movie
  • 2 : In one or more scenes, two women are present, but they’re talking about a man
  • 3 : In one or more scenes, two women have a conversation about something other than a man

Films that can answer “yes” to these two questions, or score a 3 from the above criteria are considered to pass the Bechdel test. According to this criteria, “Avengers: Endgame”, despite the abundance of male actors among the leading roles, passes the test, while “Bohemian Rhapsody”, a plot dominantly revolving around men, disqualifies.

The Bechdel Test database on bechdeltest.com, a user-edited database of some 6,500 films that evaluates the movies’ Bechdel Test results, tells us that the importance of women in the film world has not improved much in the past 30 years. Excluding the unusual low 1992, films from the late ‘80s and early ‘90s averaged around the 2.0 mark on their Bechdel Test score, while the average for 2013 films was still 2.122. While there was constant fluctuation over the late ‘90s and the 2000s, a trend or sudden point of increase was not observed in this wave.

Querying the films on the Open Movie Database with their iMDB ids, it is possible to see more specific trends based on the information in the film’s iMDB listing. When divided into films produced in the US and those in other countries, the dormancy of Bechdel scores in the U.S. was more evident. Since 2004, the average Bechdel scores of U.S. films constantly stayed in the range between 2.0 and 2.5, excluding the films in the year 2009.

Major production companies scored different on the test based on the films they produced. New Line Cinema, the Los Angeles-based production studio, scored the highest among the major production companies with a 2.59 average in Bechdel scores. Interestingly, Walt Disney Pictures, a production studio whose major audience is children, scored second-to-lowest among these companies with a 2.0 average.

This trend for weak representation of women in children-targeted films shows again when we see the average Bechdel score by genre as well. Films whose genres included animation scored an average of 1.68, a score lower than the majority of genres. Films whose plots largely involved interactions between humans, such as romance and comedy, were on the higher end of the Bechdel scores, whereas those with violence and physical action, such as crime and action, tended to score lower.

Films that were on the higher end in terms of budget tended to downsize the importance of women in their plots. Seen by the Bechdel score of the films, the films who had two women not talking to each other were budgeted the highest on average, whereas those that had at least two women having a conversation about something other than a man were on the low end in terms of average budget. The result also brings attention to the wage gap between male and female actors, which was a field of much controversy in Hollywood in the 2010s. Recently, actress Michelle Williams officially discussed the gender pay gap with the government after realizing she was paid $1,000 while her fellow actor Mark Wahlberg’s $1.5 million for their film “All the Money in the World.”
